Think of it like city planning or designing a massive skyscraper, which is reflected in the background image.You need to understand the fundamental, structures that dictate how the agent reasons,acts, and ultimately performs in the real world.Over the next few minutes,we will explore why architectural, strategy is paramount and break down the four core agent architectures, dominating the 2025 landscape.Let's get started and understand why choosing the right architecture, is a strategic imperative.The mission is clear.We must understand why architecture, is not optional in modern AI agent development.Many developers treat agents like glorified chatbots,focusing purely on prompt engineering.But the difference between a, system built without a plan and, one built with a solid architectural, foundation, is immense.It isn't a small 10% improvement.It is the difference between success and failure at scale.It distinguishes a maintainable solution from a complete debugging nightmare.And perhaps most importantly,

it separates a profitable system from one that costs a fortune in unnecessary, API calls.Your choice of architecture, is a strategic decision that dictates, every single critical aspect of your agents performance.There are six key dimensions we use to judge any agent architecture,essentially the scorecard for success.First, reasoning.How does the agent think?Is it a careful step-by-step, process or does it try to do everything all at once?Second, cost.This is paramount.We measure this primarily by the number of LLM, calls and the resulting token usage.Third, reliability.Can the system handle unexpected, paths or does it frequently crash or break when faced with novelty?Fourth, latency.How fast does it respond?This is crucial for userfacing applications.Fifth, scalability.How does the system perform when, moving from handling one task, to handling millions, simultaneously?And finally, interpretability.Can you understand why the agent made a specific decision, which is vital for auditing and compliance?

These six metrics form the foundation for evaluating, every strategy we will discuss today.To understand these agent architectures, let's use an analogy.Imagine you are leading a complex project,say organizing a highstakes operation or a complex bank heist.Your chosen strategy determines, everything from team size to communication protocol.The same principle applies to agents.An agent architecture, is the fundamental blueprint for how the system thinks and acts.We've identified four core heist strategies,each representing a primary architectural pattern.Strategy one is the improviser.Often known as rayak style, this agent adapts step by step,constantly reacting to new information as it comes.It is highly flexible but can be slow and unpredictable, because of the constant recalculations, required.Strategy two is the blueprint or plan then execute.You create a detailed, rigid plan upfront using a powerful, model and then a cheaper model, follows it precisely.It is highly efficient but brittle.

If circumstances change mid-execution, the entire plan might become invalid.Strategy three is the specialists which maps to a hierarchical architecture.Here you assemble a team, delegating, tasks to various experts and coordinating their work with a manager agent.It scales exceptionally, well for massive problems but requires significant overhead for coordination, and communication logic.Finally, strategy 4 is the labyrinth or the graph-based approach.You design a detailed flowchart with explicit decision points,conditional edges,and defined states.It is highly deterministic and resumable, offering massive reliability,but it demands that the architect anticipate, every possible path the agent might take beforehand.Understanding these four core strategies, is the first step toward becoming an effective agent architect.Let's dive into strategy one.The improviser, better known as react,which stands for reasoning plus acting.This is the most intuitive, pattern, as it mirrors human problem solving.

through a continuous feedback, loop of thought,action, and observation.The power of React lies in its natural adaptation.The feedback loop enables the, agent to adjust its next thought, and action immediately, if a tool fails or if unexpected, information arises.It is truly flexible.However, the catch is the immense cost of improvisation.Every single cycle which involves a thought, an action,and the resulting observation costs an LLM call.This makes the cost linear with the complexity of the task.A task requiring 10 cycles is 10 times the cost of a single call.At scale, this becomes unsustainable quickly.For instance, processing just 1 million tasks per day,each requiring eight calls, could potentially cost $400,000 per day,highlighting the need for optimization.Despite the cost,React is best for real-time adaptation and dynamic environments,interactive scenarios like customer support, and exploratory, tasks like research.Essentially,use React when flexibility and responsiveness.

are far more critical than minimizing API costs,such as when you need to navigate an unknown environment effectively.Moving to strategy two, the blueprint or plan then execute.This strategy, separates the agent into two distinct roles to optimize, specifically for cost and robustness.We divide the labor between the planner and the executive.The planner is a powerful expensive LLM,perhaps GPT4, or a similar model, used only once upfront to perform high-level strategic thinking.Its output is a detailed step-by-step plan.The executive in contrast is a simpler,cheaper model like Claude 3 Haiku that performs tactical execution.It follows the plan one step, at a time without needing to, reason about the overall strategy.The power of this approach is three-fold.First, massive cost efficiency as the expensive model is only called once.Second, robustness as the plan provides a clear audit trail.Third, we maximize the capabilities of the models used,optimizing.

the powerful model for planning and the cheaper model for predictable, sequential action.However, the catch is that the system is brittle.If a step fails mid-execution, the entire plan might be invalid.This requires a complex and non-trivial, replanning loop which often nullifies the initial efficiency gains.Therefore, plan then execute is best for well ststructured decomposible, problems,cost critical applications where minimizing token usage is key,multi-step tasks with clear dependencies,and any situation where auditability, of the sequence of actions is important for compliance or debugging.Strategy three tackles the inherent limits of a single agent, such as cognitive load and performance bottlenecks, by introducing the specialists or hierarchical agents.This architecture creates a multi-layered, system of manager agents and specialist agents.The manager agent receives the primary goal, decomposes it,delegates subtasks to the specialists,

and finally synthesizes their output into the final answer.The specialists such as a research agent, a data analyst agent,or a writing agent, are each optimized for their specific domain.The power here stems from specialization,allowing each agent to be fine-tuned with different models, prompts,or tool sets for its specific job.Crucially, it enables parallelization,meaning multiple specialists can work simultaneously,dramatically reducing latency compared to a sequential react loop.And finally, scalability.The system naturally scales to handle enormous problems, by simply adding more specialists or new management layers.The catch is the coordination overhead.Managing this team requires complex logic for specialist selection, delegation strategies and robust inter agent communication.This added complexity introduces potential latency, due to communication rounds and makes the system harder to debug initially.Hierarchical agents are best for enterprise scale complexity,

problems that have clear and separable domains,situations where parallelization is absolutely required for maximum performance,and large systems needing clear governance and audit trails for specialized, actions.Our final architectural approach is strategy 4,the labyrinth or graph-based orchestration.This method treats the entire agent system as a finite state machine, defined by explicit states,nodes, and conditional edges.This design enables deterministic and fully resumable workflows.The architecture relies on three core components.Nodes, which are the functions,tools or agents that perform an action.State,a shared data structure that persists through the entire workflow, holding all intermediate results.and edges, the conditional connections that define the flow based on the current state.The power which we call the kick is unmatched reliability.Because the state is explicit and managed,the workflow is possible and resumable.This is critical for long running, processes and workflows that.

require human in the loop interaction, like an approval step.It provides unmatched reliability, because you always know exactly where you are and how to restart.The catch, however,is the architect's burden.The architect must anticipate, and define all possible states and transitions up front.This sacrifices the spontaneous adaptability, you find in React as the agent is constrained by the predefined graph flow.Graph-based orchestration, is best for complex branching and heavy conditional logic,long running stateful processes,workflows that inherently require human involvement, and highstakes production systems where reliability and auditability, are absolutely paramount, such as financial transaction processing.Before comparing the four core architectures,we need to address a fifth technique, the forger's instinct or tool firmer.This is not an architecture, but a different technique for tool utilization that often coexists, with architectural, choices.

In React, the agent decides to use a tool based on reasoning.It is an explicit instruction.With tool firmer,the LLM is fine-tuned to generate tool calls directly within its text output,learning implicitly when a tool will improve its response.The power of this approach lies in learned tool use.The model learns how to use tools implicitly from data, rather than through explicit prompting mechanisms.This can be highly efficient because the tool calls are integrated seamlessly, and directly into the generation stream.The LLM essentially forges the tool use into its output.The catch, however,is the high barrier to entry, the need for retraining.This approach fundamentally requires fine-tuning the underlying LLM, model itself.It cannot be applied to pre-trained, API only models like GPT4 where you lack access to the weights.Furthermore,adding new tools or significantly changing existing tools, often requires completely retraining the model.

Tool firmer is best for situations where you can fine-tune your own model, when tool use is a learned behavior rather than an instructed one, where you have stable and unchanging, tool sets, and for specialized, domain models focusing on specific tasks like complex calculation, or information retrieval.Let's perform the debrief a head-to-head strategy comparison.This table summarizes the critical tradeoffs across our five strategies, using the six metrics we defined earlier.Looking at cost efficiency,plan then execute and tool firmer are the winners, because they minimize the calls to the most expensive, models.React is low because every step is a costly decision for adaptation to change.React is excellent, living up to its name, while all others are limited, because they rely on upfront, planning or rigid graphs.Reasoning transparency is high for react, plan then execute and graph-based approaches, as they externalize their thought process.Tool firmer scores low here because, the reasoning for tool use is.

embedded implicitly, within the model weights.Scalability is where hierarchical, agents shine scoring excellent due to their ability to parallelize tasks.Finally, implementation complexity.React is the easiest, scoring low while the complex coordination required for hierarchical, graph-based and tool firmer architectures results in high complexity.This analysis reveals the core trade-offs.React wins for adaptability and simplicity.Plan then execute and tool firmer win for cost efficiency.Hierarchical wins for scalability, and graph-based orchestration wins for reliability.These are not objective rankings.They are strategic choices, based on what constraint matters most to your mission.Now for the ultimate question, the architect's choice.How do you match the strategy to the mission?We can use this flowchart as a decision guide.Start at the top left.First question, is your environment dynamic and unpredictable?If no, you move to optimizing structure and cost?

If yes, you move down to the next question.Do you need real-time adaptation above all else?If yes, your answer is clear.Use React.If the environment is not dynamic,we ask is the problem well structured and decomposable?If yes, we move to cost.Is cost minimization a primary driver?If yes, plan then execute is your answer.If the problem is highly complex, we move to the hierarchical path.Do you need to manage extreme complexity with many specialized tasks?If yes, is parallelization beneficial?If the answer is yes, use hierarchical agents.If you need robust workflows, we follow the graph path.Is the workflow long running with complex branching and a need for human input?If yes, is production reliability and resumability critical?If yes, use graph-based orchestration.Finally, for tool firmer,the question is always about access.Can you fine-tune your own model?And is tool use a learned behavior you want to embed?If yes to both, use toolmer.

Following this logical path ensures your architectural choice aligns perfectly, with the performance constraints of your specific use case.Choosing an architecture is merely the first strategic step.Next, you need the right tools,the arsenal to build it.The framework landscape in 2025 is dominated by a few key players,each built with a distinct philosophy and a specialized set of strengths.We will examine six of the most prominent frameworks.Open AAI agents SDK which offers a minimalist path.The powerful combination of lang, chain plus lang graph giving, you ultimate flexibility.Autogen focused on conversational multi-, aent systems.Crew AI designed for intuitive role-based teamwork.Semantic kernel Microsoft's enterprisegrade orchestration tool and N8N, a visual automation tool perfect for non-technical, users and human in the loop workflows.Your choice of framework is just as strategic as your choice of architecture.It is not just a matter of coding preference.

It directly impacts your development speed,the flexibility you have to customize complex workflows,and ultimately the long-term scalability of your agent system.Using the wrong framework for a graph-based architecture, for instance,can lead to needless complexity and debugging headaches.Let's analyze these frameworks in detail,dividing them into the engineers toolkit and the enterprise toolkit.Starting with the engineers toolkit.These frameworks are typically favored by developers comfortable with low-level, code and maximum customization.First, the OpenAI agents SDK.Its philosophy is minimalist, focusing on only four core primitives,challenging you to build the rest yourself.It is best for simple React loops and rapid prototyping,especially if you're exclusively using OpenAI models.Its strengths are its simplicity, making it the easiest to learn,and its tight integration with the OpenAI ecosystem.Its main weakness is the lack of native graph orchestration or managed memory,

limiting complex workflows.Next, we have Lang Chain plus Lang Graph.Langchain's philosophy is to be the comprehensive ecosystem, aiming to solve any LLM problem you encounter.Lang graph then extends this capability to explicitly, support graph-based state machines.It is best for complex, production grade systems that require robust graph-based state machines.Its strengths include extreme flexibility, excellent graph support,and superior debugging capabilities thanks to its explicit chain visualization.Its weakness is a steep learning curve.It can often be overkill for simple or lightweight tasks.Finally, autogen.Its philosophy, centers on agents as conversational entities that interact via messages,fostering natural communication between them.It is best for multi-agent research, simulation,and collaborative workflows.Its strengths are its excellence in multi-agent collaboration, and its highly natural conversation-based, paradigm.Its weakness is that it is less suitable for strict deterministic.

workflows and offers more limited state management compared to lang graph.Moving on to the team and enterprise toolkit.These frameworks prioritize structured flows, governance,and accessibility.First, crew AI.Its philosophy is to build AI, teams where each agent has a, specific role and works collaboratively, to achieve a goal.It is best for role-based teamwork, content creation,and teams that are new to agentic development.Its strengths are its intuitive role-based design,making it highly beginnerfriendly and quick to implement.Its weakness is that it is less, flexible than lang chain for, building highly complex or customized, architectures, outside of its predefined team structure.Second, semantic kernel.This is Microsoft's answer and its philosophy is focused on enterprisegrade, orchestration with highly structured and predictable flows.It is best for enterprise organizations,especially those aligned with Microsoft's Azure ecosystem, and structured workflows that require specific governance.

Its strengths include deep Azure integration, and the use of a dependency injection, pattern which promotes very clean architecture.Its weakness is a steeper learning, curve than crew AI and weaker, native support for complex, loosely coupled multi-, aent systems.Finally, N8N.Its philosophy is visual accessible automation designed for non-technical teams.It is built to treat agent systems as part of a wider automation process.It is best for business users,general workflow automation, and processes that require human in the loop intervention.Its strengths are its noode visual interface making it easy for non-developers, and its incredibly strong integration ecosystem boasting over 500 connectors.Its weakness is that it is less suitable for complex, deep code level agent reasoning or highly abstract tasks.Let's cement our knowledge by, applying the theory to four real, world mission scenarios.First consider a customer support chatbot.The needs are real time interactive responsiveness and high adaptability.

Therefore,the ideal architecture is React, chosen specifically for its flexibility in handling unpredictable user requests.The frameworks best suited are the OpenAI, agents SDK or Crew AI, both of which offer speed and simplicity for building a robust, adaptive conversational, loop.Second, a data analysis pipeline.The needs here are multi-step execution being cost-sensitive, and requiring high reliability.The architecture must combine efficiency and robustness.We choose plan then execute for upfront efficiency, but crucially run it on a graph for reliability.The frameworks, lang chain plus lang graph or semantic kernel, as both offer the necessary tools for robust deterministic, flows that can manage state across multiple steps and dependencies.Third, a research team simulation.This demands multiple specialists, collaboration,and deep exploration.The natural architecture is hierarchical,chosen for its specialization capabilities.The frameworks are autogen or crew AI,

both excelling at managing and coordinating multi-, aent collaboration seamlessly.Finally, an enterprise insurance claim workflow.The needs are complex branching, mandatory human approval steps,a clear audit trail and often operation by non-technical users.The architecture must be graph-based, chosen for its stateful management of human in the loop processes, and its inherent auditability.The framework choice is N8N, which provides a visual no code workflow builder that accommodates, both complex flow control and broad integration needs.Perfect for largecale enterprise automation.We reach the extraction.Architecture is strategy.It is crucial to internalize that agentic architectures, are not merely academic concepts.They are strategic choices that inherently create trade-offs.Your primary job as an architect, is to understand and navigate, these choices effectively.We can visualize these trade-offs in four key quadrants.First, determinism versus flexibility.

Do you prioritize a predictable and reliable system, or one that is adaptive and surprising?Second, latency versus reasoning depth.Do you need a fast and cheap response or a slow,resource-heavy but highquality reasoning output?Third, cost versus quality.Do you aim for a budget-friendly, solution that is merely adequate or an expensive system that is truly excellent?Fourth, control versus autonomy.Do you require human in the loop safety and governance, or are you aiming for a fully autonomous and potentially riskier operation?Successfully building agents requires adopting the practitioner's mindset,a four-step process.Step one, understand the problem.Define your primary constraints up front such as cost, quality,speed, or reliability.Step two, match to architecture.Choose the blueprint, whether react,plan, then execute,hierarchical, or graph that best aligns with those defined constraints.Step three, choose a framework.Select the tool, be it lang chain,autogen, or semantic kernel,

that best fits your team's skills and your chosen architecture.Step four, prototype,validate, iterate,build the system, measure its performance against the six key metrics,and refine the design.This systematic, approach is the difference between building agents by accident and architecting, true intelligence with purpose and precision.Thank you for watching and let us know what you think in the comments.
